In this article, we explore the history of ketchup: by whom and when the ketchup was invented, what the original ketchup recipe was, and how this source influences our everyday life and even culture.



The first ketchup recipe included neither tomatoes, vinegar, nor many other ingredients that make ketchup well… ketchup. The first base of the sauce was fish, then oysters, mushrooms, and only after that – tomatoes.



When was Ketchup Invented, and Who Did It?

Ketchup has a surprisingly long evolution that originated in China. The first version was based on pickled fish and looked more like a soy sauce – with a dark and thin texture. It was called “keh-jup” or “koe-cheup,” meaning “fish sauce.” Thanks to the fermentation, the sauce was easy to conserve and could make a long journey. So, eventually, at the beginning of the 18th century, British and Dutch merchants brought ketchup to Europe. There, the cooks tried to recreate the recipe and, as a result, added some new ingredients. But it was still far from the dip we know today.


The first known printed recipe was in Eliza Smith’s Compleat Housewife, first published in 1727
